It happened to me once – I lost my small 64GB USB drive I’d used for work. I was pretty sure it had nothing too important on it. But then I started wondering… what if it contains some sensitive files, perhaps a copy of my national ID card or driver's license? Probably some important scanned documents? 

That was the moment I realized that USB drives are small, portable, and ridiculously easy to lose, and storing any unencrypted data on them can be dangerous to my privacy. I’d heard of BitLocker, of course. It works well, but only on certain editions of Windows. And even on supported editions, it’s a bit rigid when it comes to removable drives.

That’s when I began searching for a better solution. Something flexible, free, and strong enough to protect personal data without dragging down performance. And after trying a few options, I finally discovered a seriously underrated gem – [DiskCryptor](www.diskcryptor.com).

## What is DiskCryptor?
### A free, powerful encryption tool almost no one talks about
DiskCryptor is an open-source encryption utility that lets you fully encrypt USB drives, external hard drives, and even your system drives. It isn’t flashy, and it’s not from a big-name company, but it gets the job done better than most tools I’ve used.

It’s lightweight, no-nonsense, and works even on older machines. If you want serious security without the bloat or licensing restrictions, this is the tool that quietly outperforms its competitors.
Unlike some encryption programs that only protect individual files or folders, DiskCryptor encrypts the entire volume. That means even if someone tries to scan the drive using another OS or tool, they won't be able to access – unless, of course, they have your password.

What I like about DiskCryptor (and why I keep using it)
It’s fast, reliable, and doesn’t get in the way
Full drive encryption without limitations
You don’t need Windows 10 Pro, TPM chips, or enterprise security features. DiskCryptor just works. Whether it's a cheap flash drive or a high-capacity SSD, you can encrypt it without jumping through hoops. I’ve used it on everything from small thumb drives to multi-terabyte internal and external SSDs and HDDs.
Lightweight and fast, even on old machines
I’ve used DiskCryptor on a decade-old laptop. It doesn’t slow it down or hog system resources. Once the encryption is done, you won’t even notice it’s running. During testing, the encrypted USB drive performed nearly identically to its unencrypted state once mounted. That’s not something I can say for all encryption tools.
Lets me choose an encryption algorithm of my choice
DiskCryptor supports AES, Twofish, and Serpent encryption algorithms. You can select one or even layer them for added strength. Whether you want blazing-fast AES performance or prefer Serpent for its theoretical robustness, DiskCryptor gives you options. You’re not locked into one approach, and that freedom matters if you’re concerned about long-term data resilience.
Rate your password before encrypting the drive
One feature I found particularly useful is that DiskCryptor evaluates the strength of your password before it starts encrypting. It’s a simple addition, but one that nudges you toward creating something truly secure. That little nudge will prompt you to ditch your lazy 8-character password and actually force you to think about creating something safer.
Boots with a password prompt (if you want)
If you choose to encrypt your system drive, DiskCryptor can prompt for a password before Windows even loads. That’s a serious layer of protection against unauthorized access and something few tools outside of enterprise-grade options offer.
Imagine if your device is ever stolen, this feature prevents someone from even booting into Windows without the correct password. And since DiskCryptor encrypts the entire drive, recovery tools and forensic software are essentially useless without the decryption key.
How I used DiskCryptor to encrypt my USB drive
A simple process, and totally worth it
Here’s how I encrypted my USB drive using DiskCryptor:
Note: You’ll want to back up anything important beforehand, because encryption wipes the drive during setup.
Download DiskCryptor from the official website or directly from its GitHub page.
Install it and launch the program. 
Select your USB drive from the list, or right-click on it and select Encrypt. 
Choose your preferred algorithm and wipe mode. 
Next, create a strong password and confirm it. Check the password strength before confirming it.
Confirm and wait while the entire volume is encrypted. Depending on the encryption algorithm, disk type, disk size, and your system, it can take anywhere from 20 minutes to even hours.
That’s it. The whole process took about 25 minutes on a 32GB drive. Once encrypted, the drive is unreadable unless you mount it through DiskCryptor and enter the password.
A few things to keep in mind
It’s not perfect, but the pros outweigh the cons
Like any tool, DiskCryptor has its caveats:
It’s Windows-only. There’s no native macOS or Linux support.
The project is stable, but it is not under active development. The latest beta version was released in February 2024, and the stable version was released in October 2023.
You’ll need administrator rights to set it up and mount encrypted drives.
Encrypted drives can’t be opened on other machines unless they also have DiskCryptor installed. You need the app to mount or decrypt the disk using your set password.
There’s no way to recover your data if you forget your password. There’s no way to recover or reset your password either.
If someone gains access to your encrypted USB disk, they won’t be able to access the data it contains; however, they can format the drive and reuse it for their own purposes.
None of these are dealbreakers for me, especially since I use it on drives I only access from trusted machines. But it’s worth knowing before you dive in.
